Every company owns things — factories, inventory, patents, cash. All of it is funded one of two ways: money the company borrowed, or money the owners put in (plus the profits they've left inside the business). The first bucket is **liabilities**. The second is **equity**. Together they always equal the full value of what the company owns — that's the balance sheet equation.

The **liabilities-to-equity ratio** just divides one by the other. A company with $100 of debt and $100 of equity has a ratio of 1. At $200 of debt against $50 of equity, it's 4. The higher the number, the more the company is leaning on borrowed money to operate.

For value investors, this is a crude but useful risk check. A low ratio means the business can absorb a bad year without creditors losing patience. A high one means interest payments keep coming whether sales do or not — and in a downturn, that's how companies fail. Same assets, same revenue, very different fragility.

One thing worth knowing: the "right" ratio depends on the industry. Banks and utilities run high by design; software companies often run near zero. Comparing a retailer to a railroad on this metric tells you nothing. Comparing two retailers tells you a lot.

On Sponda you can see the liabilities-to-equity ratio for any company, alongside the underlying figures it's built from, so you can tell at a glance whether a cheap-looking stock is cheap because it's out of favor or because it's carrying too much debt.
